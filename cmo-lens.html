What actually keeps them up at night

01
Nobody can tell us apart
61% of tech CMOs struggle to create distinctive brand narratives that buyers recognize and repeat. Everyone sounds the same. The brand has no point of view the market can hold onto.
02
I can't prove any of this works
74% face rising ROI pressure. Only 30% have a clear definition of what marketing ROI even means. None of 200+ senior leaders surveyed by McKinsey could clearly articulate their MarTech ROI.
03
The CEO doesn't understand what we do
CEO-CMO misalignment increased 20% from 2023-2025. Only 32% of executives say their CMO makes compelling strategy recommendations based on market data. The CMO is fighting for a seat they're supposed to already have.
04
AI is changing everything and I'm not ready
62% lack skills, budget, or resources to compete on AI search. Organic visibility is declining. The playbook that worked for a decade is eroding and the replacement isn't clear yet.
05
Everyone wants something different from me
94% struggle to translate enterprise strategy into actionable plans. The CEO wants growth. The CFO wants attribution. Sales wants leads. Product wants positioning. The board wants all of it. "Strategic dysfunction" — unclear, conflicting objectives that marketing must somehow serve.

Where content professionals fit
Market shaping is a narrative act. Someone has to build the story architecture, develop the proof points, write the positioning, craft the keynotes, shape how the company talks about itself and its category. That someone is often a content professional operating at the highest level — whether they carry a content title or not. The CMO sets the direction. The narrative professional builds the vehicle.

The two narratives

Every marketing organization needs two narratives running in parallel. Most only build one.

External narrative
How the market sees the brand
Demand generation and pipeline
Product positioning and messaging
Customer-facing content, campaigns, events
Conference presentations
Internal narrative
How the organisation understands itself
Positioning documents and memos
Team alignment and shared vocabulary
Board presentations and executive comms
Cross-functional alignment (sales, product, finance)

The 84% strategic dysfunction stat is essentially: the internal narrative is broken. Teams aren't aligned. The CMO can't translate enterprise objectives into a coherent story that everyone rows behind. The external brand suffers because the internal house is in disorder.

The best CMOs are called "Chief Meaning Officers" — they create unified narratives amid fragmentation and competing priorities. Both narratives are made of the same raw material: words, structure, positioning, story. Both are content problems at their core.

What each stakeholder wants from the CMO

CEO
Growth and market position. "Is marketing driving the business?" 70% measure marketing impact by revenue growth and margin. Wants a strategic sparring partner, not a campaign manager.
CFO
Attribution and efficiency. "Prove every dollar." CFO pressure on CMOs increased 52% from 2023-2025. Every budget line is interrogated.
Board
Long-term brand value vs. short-term returns. Board pressure rose 21%. When profits fall short, marketing is cut first. CMOs must frame brand as a commercial asset, not an expense.
Sales
Pipeline and enablement. "Give me content that helps me close." Only 24% of tech CMOs effectively enable sales with content.
Product
Positioning and go-to-market. How the product story meets the market. CMOs who co-develop strategy with product last longer.
Team
Clarity and direction. Burnout is the #1 concern (25.9%). Workload satisfaction at 51%. Only 42% feel adequately staffed. The team needs the CMO to protect priorities, not add to the pile.

Why CMOs get fired

01
Can't demonstrate revenue contribution
The board question: "If given $Xm, where would you invest it and what incremental revenue would result?" If you can't answer, you're replaceable.
02
Lack of market and customer insight
CEOs want CMOs who can "strategically spar on company, product, and market strategy." Not just deliver demand gen numbers — shape the company's understanding of the market.
03
Eroded trust with the C-suite
Even mediocre performance can be tolerated if trust exists. Without it, you're gone. Trust is built by speaking business language, not marketing language.
"Almost every exceptional CMO has faced termination. It's not a sign of failure — it's the nature of the role." — Carilu Dietrich

What separates market shapers from enterprise operators

They stop talking marketing and start talking business. They treat internal alignment as seriously as external brand. They convert trends into strategy rather than reacting to objectives set by others. And they use narrative — not just data — to build trust with the C-suite.

Writing through the CMO lens

The goal isn't to write as a CMO or for CMOs exclusively. It's to write as someone who works with words and narrative, sees patterns in how organisations communicate, and names them. The test: would a CMO forward this to their team?

Raise the stakes
Tier 2 → 3
Take a craft insight and ask: what does this cost the business? "Content quality" becomes "brand differentiation." "Editorial standards" become "whether the market can tell you apart."
Before: Why helpful content gets forgotten
After: Why helpful content gets forgotten — and what that costs your brand when AI is the one remembering
Name the pattern
Tier 3
Find a market mechanism or widely held assumption and reframe it. Don't tell people how to do their job — tell them they've been measuring the wrong thing, solving the wrong problem, or missing the structural shift underneath.
Model: The Impression Economy — impressions always had value; clicks obscured it. The IC Ceiling — the market already pays for three tiers; nobody named them.
Use business language
Tier 3
CMOs live in a world of revenue, margin, attribution, pipeline, and board presentations. Content that mirrors that frame is credible. Content that stays in "content metrics" (traffic, rankings, engagement) doesn't reach the room where decisions are made.
Instead of: "Your content library is undervalued"
Try: "Your content library is a depreciating asset and nobody's on the balance sheet watching it"
Acknowledge the political reality
Tier 3
Content that pretends the CMO just needs "better strategy" doesn't resonate. They know the system is broken — 84% dysfunction, 65% trust deficit, conflicting objectives from every direction. Naming this reality earns credibility instantly.
Signal: "The differentiation problem isn't a creative problem. It's an alignment problem — and most organisations don't have the internal narrative to solve it."
Ground it in research
Tier 3
Your credibility comes from mapping patterns with evidence, not from lived C-suite experience. Proprietary data, real JDs, market studies, primary research — these give you permission to make claims at a level your title alone wouldn't. The IC Ceiling is the model.
The approach: Research-backed pattern recognition. You're the translator on the expertise spectrum — deep enough to see it, skilled enough to name it, rigorous enough to back it up.

The voice

You don't speak as a CMO. You don't speak to CMOs specifically. You speak as someone who works with words and narrative, who sees patterns in how organisations communicate, and who names them.

The IC Ceiling proves this works. You wrote it as a practitioner who did research and mapped a pattern the market hadn't named. A CMO reads it and thinks "this person understands the market." An IC reads it and thinks "this person just handed me a career map." Both are right.

The pattern namer who works with words. Not trying on anyone else's dress. Wearing yours and walking into bigger rooms.
