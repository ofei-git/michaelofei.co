Deep Dive · Practice 02: Map

Context-First Content Planning

How to translate customer context into a plan that both humans and AI agents can work with. Replacing keyword-first spreadsheets with a structure that represents the experience you're building — not just the pages you're publishing.

v0.3 · April 2026 · Michael Ofei

The Spreadsheet That Needs to Change

Every SEO team has the same planning spreadsheet. Rows of keywords, columns for search volume, difficulty, assigned URL, status. The organizing principle is search volume. The question it answers is "what should we rank for?"

This sheet was designed for a world where users typed unnatural keyword phrases into Google because that's what the system demanded. "Best project management tool SaaS" isn't how anyone talks. It's how people learned to talk to search engines.

AI systems removed that constraint. People interact with AI the way they talk to a knowledgeable colleague — with full context, natural language, and the expectation of a personalized answer. The search volume of most real prompts is one.

The spreadsheet needs to answer a different question: "What experience do we need to build so that AI systems can connect us to the right customers in the right situations?"

What Changes

The shift isn't just swapping keywords for something else. It's changing what a row represents, what the columns measure, and what question the entire sheet answers.

Two Layers of Planning

The context-first spreadsheet works at two levels. Layer 1 is the strategic view (which customer worlds are we investing in?). Layer 2 is the context map (what experience are we building for each customer world?). If you need a coverage audit, filter or pivot Layer 2 by surface — but that's a spreadsheet technique, not a separate artifact.

Layer 1 is a single sheet. Layer 2 is one tab per priority customer world.

"Key Surfaces" and "Current Focus" are the management columns. Key Surfaces captures the surface selection decision — which platforms matter for this customer world (partly industry-dependent, partly customer-world-dependent). Current Focus is forward-looking: where is energy going right now? Status reflects maturity, not completion — customer worlds move from Researching to Building to Active to Extending, but they never reach "done." Selection comes from Practice 1 (Know): which customer worlds have the most distinct problem contexts and the most commercial potential?

Six columns, each doing distinct work:

Column What It Captures Why It Matters
Problem Context The situation the customer brings to AI Replaces "keyword" as the row identity. Comes from Practice 1 (Know). Not a search term — a human situation. The Narrative row captures the overarching tension for the customer world; other rows capture specific problems.
Component Narrative, Proof, Utility, or Distribution A tag, not the organizing principle. Tells you what type of experience component this row is and what job it does in the overall experience.
Asset The specific content or action needed What you're actually building. Concrete enough to assign and track.
Surfaces Where it lives (multi-select: site, YouTube, TikTok, G2, Reddit...) Not every component lives on your website. The available surfaces come from the Key Surfaces decision in Layer 1. Place the right thing on the right surface.
Demand Signal Any evidence that people care about this problem Validation, not the organizing principle. Goes beyond search volume: video views, social engagement, support ticket frequency, sales call mentions. Some rows don't need demand data to justify existing.
Status Not started, planned, draft, published, monitoring, ongoing Execution tracking. "Monitoring" and "Ongoing" are important — review platforms, Reddit, and social surfaces don't have a "published" state.

Optional columns depending on team size: Owner, Last Updated, URL, Notes. Keep it lean — the point is planning clarity, not tracking overhead.

The Experience Blueprint

The context map isn't a content calendar or a to-do list. It's the blueprint for how your brand shows up in one customer's world — across every surface where they or their AI agent might encounter you.

Think about what happens when someone asks an AI assistant for a recommendation. The AI doesn't land on one page and read top to bottom. It assembles a picture from everything it finds across the web — your product pages, video reviews, community threads, review platform ratings, structured data. It builds its own first impression, in real time, from signals you've placed across surfaces.

Your context map is the blueprint for what that agent assembles. Every row is a signal. The more complete and consistent those signals are, the more confidently the agent can connect your brand to that person's situation.

The Blueprint
The Experience for This Customer World
Every component you build — every case study, template, video, review, community thread — adds to the picture that humans and AI agents use to understand your brand for this customer's situation.
What the human sees
One surface at a time
They land on your site, find your video, read a review. Each touchpoint should feel like you understand their specific world.
A PM sees templates built for their workflow. A yoga practitioner sees fabrics tested for their practice. A couple sees photos from evenings like theirs.
What the AI agent sees
All surfaces at once
The agent assembles a complete picture from everything you've published. Dense, consistent signals across surfaces mean confident recommendations.
Product pages + customer reviews + community threads + video content + structured data = enough context to match your brand to the right situation.
What the context map does
Designs both experiences
Every row serves the human and gives the AI agent more context to work with. The map is the blueprint for both experiences simultaneously.
The form varies by industry — a SaaS hub page, a DTC collection, a restaurant profile — but the job is always the same: make this customer feel understood.

The sophistication is in the density. When someone's AI agent encounters your brand across multiple surfaces — and finds consistent, specific, context-rich information at every turn — it can make a confident recommendation. When it finds thin presence on one surface and silence everywhere else, it moves on to a brand that gave it more to work with.

The context map makes this tangible. You can look at it and ask: if an AI agent assembled everything in this map right now, would it have enough to confidently recommend us for this customer's situation? If the answer is no, the gaps are visible. That's the planning power of the map.

Four Component Types

Every row in the context map is one of four types. Each has a different job in the experience you're building. (Machine-readable entity signals — schema, structured data, knowledge base entries — live in Practice 3: Structure. They're infrastructure that makes all four components parseable, not a content type of their own.)

Narrative
The source of truth. The anchor for this customer world. Everything else points back to it or extends from it.
SaaS: "for [audience]" hub page. DTC: curated collection + editorial. Hospitality: experience page + Google Business Profile. Services: vertical-specific service page.
Proof
Evidence that you deliver for this customer. Makes the narrative credible. Lives on owned site and external surfaces.
SaaS: case studies, G2 reviews. DTC: UGC try-on content, Trustpilot. Hospitality: Google reviews, food critic features. Services: client testimonials, portfolio pieces.
Utility
Practical tools and resources that solve real problems for this customer. The attraction and retention engine.
SaaS: templates, integration guides. DTC: size guides, outfit builders, care instructions. Hospitality: menus, dietary filters, reservation tools. Services: calculators, audit tools, checklists.
Distribution
Your story told on surfaces you don't own. Extends brand presence to where AI agents and customers discover information.
SaaS: YouTube walkthroughs, Reddit participation. DTC: TikTok, Instagram, Pinterest. Hospitality: local food blogs, Instagram reels. Services: podcast appearances, guest articles, industry forums.

What This Changes About Content Planning

Choosing your customer worlds becomes the first strategic decision
You can't build this depth for every audience simultaneously. Choosing which customer worlds to invest in — and which to defer — is the most consequential decision. It comes from Practice 1 (Know): which customer worlds have problem contexts distinct enough to warrant their own experience?
Broad content still exists, but it's no longer the whole strategy
A general "best running shoes" article or "SEO strategy" guide that ranks for the head term still has value. But it's now the general-purpose layer — not the strategy itself. The strategic depth lives in the customer-world-specific experiences. The broad piece introduces. The specific experience converts.
Not every row needs a search volume to justify existing
A Reddit participation strategy has no keyword volume. A TikTok review has no organic search potential. An Instagram community has no ranking to track. But all three build the multi-surface presence that AI agents use when making recommendations. Demand signals validate; they don't determine.
Progress metrics shift from rankings to coverage and depth
"How many of the top 10 do we hold?" becomes "How many problem contexts have we covered for this customer world? How many surfaces are we present on? How complete is the picture an AI agent would assemble?" Context Depth and Surface Coverage are the new KPIs.
Teams need a wider surface mix
The keyword spreadsheet needed writers and SEO strategists. The context map also needs people managing community presence, review platforms, video content, and social surfaces. The specific roles depend on your industry — a DTC brand needs TikTok and Instagram expertise, a SaaS company needs forum and review site presence, a restaurant needs Google Business Profile management — but the pattern is the same: the surface mix is wider than most teams are structured for.
