Deep Dive · Practice 03: Structure

Make Your Brand Machine-Readable

How to articulate what your brand is, audit whether AI systems agree, and encode your identity into the infrastructure they read. The practice that turns brand consistency into a visibility deliverable.

v0.3 · April 2026 · Michael Ofei

The Identity Fragmentation Problem

Most brands describe themselves differently on every surface they occupy. Their G2 profile calls them a "project management tool." Wikidata says "note-taking application." Crunchbase still has the description from their Series A. LinkedIn sounds like HR wrote it. TikTok sounds like an intern wrote it. Each description was written by a different person, at a different time, for a different audience — and nobody coordinates because nobody had to.

Before AI search, this didn't matter for visibility. Your Trustpilot description had nothing to do with your search rankings. Your TikTok bio existed in a completely separate universe from your schema markup. Google read your website. Everything else was a branding concern.

AI search changed the equation. AI systems don't just read your website — they assemble your brand identity from every surface simultaneously. Your G2 reviews, your Wikidata entry, your YouTube channel description, your social bios, your community mentions. When those surfaces tell different stories about what you are, the AI's confidence drops. And when it's not confident about what you are, it's not confident recommending you.

The only precedent is NAP consistency in local SEO — making sure your Name, Address, and Phone number were identical across every directory. But NAP was mechanical: copy your address correctly across 50 listings. This is strategic: articulate what your brand is, then make sure every surface AI reads agrees. Brand narrative consistency became an SEO deliverable. Most teams haven't caught up.

A note on technical accessibility: This practice assumes your content is technically accessible to AI systems — that crawlers can reach and render your pages, that your site loads and functions properly, that basic technical SEO foundations are in place. If AI systems can't access your content, entity signals don't matter. Technical accessibility (crawlability, rendering, emerging AI-specific protocols) is foundational infrastructure this workflow builds on top of, not a replacement for.

Two Outputs

Structure produces the identity foundation that Publish builds on and Measure audits. Without the first output, the second is just technical markup with no strategic backbone. Without the second, your identity exists in a Google Doc that AI systems can't read.

01
Defined Brand Narratives
The canonical articulation of what your brand is — at company level and for each priority customer world. One paragraph each. These are the source of truth that every schema description, every profile bio, every piece of content, and every community interaction draws from.
Company-level answers "what are we?" for AI systems and humans alike. Customer-world-level answers "what are we for this specific customer?" Both need to exist before encoding makes any sense.
Feeds into: the encoding step below (what you encode), Practice 2 (Map) retroactively sharpens the Narrative component row, and Practice 4 (Publish) as the narrative foundation for all content and presence.
02
Consistent Entity Infrastructure
Machine-readable identity across all surfaces AI reads. Schema markup on your site, Knowledge Graph presence, profile descriptions on review platforms, social bios, business directories. Not just markup on your website — consistency across every surface where your brand is described.
The infrastructure that makes your brand parseable. Content is published onto this foundation. Without it, even great content sits on a brand that AI systems can't confidently identify or categorize.
Feeds into: Practice 4 (Publish) builds content on this foundation. Practice 5 (Measure) uses this as the baseline for ongoing consistency audits.

Why This Matters Now

Before AI search, brand consistency lived in a brand guidelines PDF that marketing referenced and everyone else ignored. SEO teams managed your website. Social teams managed your bios. Product teams wrote G2 responses. Nobody coordinated because the surfaces didn't talk to each other — your Wikidata entry had nothing to do with your organic rankings.

AI systems changed this fundamentally. When someone asks an AI for a recommendation, the system pulls from every surface where your brand appears — simultaneously. It reads your schema markup, your review platform profiles, your social bios, your Wikipedia entry, your community mentions. It assembles a composite identity in real time. If those sources agree, the AI's entity confidence is high. If they disagree, the AI hedges — or moves on to a brand that gave it clearer signals.

This means every surface where your brand is described is now an input to your AI search visibility. Your Trustpilot profile description. Your TikTok bio. Your Wikidata entry. Your Crunchbase short description. None of these were SEO concerns before. They all are now.

The Shift
Brand narrative consistency became an SEO deliverable the moment AI systems started reading every surface to understand what your brand is.

Step 1: Define Your Narratives

Before you can encode your identity or audit your consistency, you need to know what you're being consistent about. Most teams skip this step — they go straight to schema markup or content creation without ever writing down what their brand actually is at each level. Then they wonder why AI systems describe them differently on every surface.

The definition work happens at two levels: company and customer world. The output is lightweight — one paragraph each. The hard part isn't writing the paragraph. It's getting organizational alignment on a single version.

Company-Level Narrative

Three questions produce one paragraph. This paragraph becomes the master description that every schema entry, every profile bio, and every About section draws from. If your company had to introduce itself in one breath to an AI system that had never encountered you, this is what you'd say.

01
What are we?
The category. Not marketing language — the honest answer. This goes in your schema "description," your Wikidata "instance of" field, and the first sentence of every profile.
SaaS: "Connected workspace." DTC: "Activewear brand." Hospitality: "Farm-to-table restaurant." Services: "Digital marketing agency."
02
What do we do?
The core value. What someone gets from you that they can't get from doing nothing. Specific enough that an AI system could distinguish you from your category peers.
SaaS: "Replace scattered tools with one system that adapts to how each team works." DTC: "Studio-to-street performance at half the luxury price." Hospitality: "Seasonal tasting menus using local ingredients." Services: "Content-led SEO for B2B SaaS companies."
03
Who are we for?
The audience, in situation terms. Not demographics — the customer worlds from Know. Broad enough to cover your brand, specific enough to be useful.
SaaS: "Teams replacing tool sprawl." DTC: "People who want their workout wardrobe to work as hard as they do." Hospitality: "Couples and small groups seeking memorable dining." Services: "B2B SaaS companies scaling organic growth."

Combine into one paragraph. Two examples:

Brand Canonical Narrative
Notion (SaaS) Notion is a connected workspace where teams organize work, manage projects, and build shared knowledge — replacing scattered tools with one system that adapts to how each team works.
Adanola (DTC) Adanola is a UK-based activewear brand making performance-driven pieces that transition from studio to street — designed for people who want their workout wardrobe to work as hard as they do, without the luxury price tag.

This is the paragraph that matters more than any schema implementation. If this paragraph is clear and everyone in the organization uses it, your entity consistency is already ahead of 90% of brands. If this paragraph doesn't exist — or if marketing has one version, sales has another, and product has a third — no amount of structured data will fix the fragmentation downstream.

The Entity Density Pass

The three-question framework produces clean, human-readable narratives. But clean isn't enough. AI systems extract entity relationships from your descriptions — brand names, product names, competitive alternatives, integrations, specific use cases. Abstract language like "helps teams organize work" creates weak entity signals. Concrete language that names specific entities creates the relationships AI systems use to categorize and recommend you.

Once you have the clean narrative, run an entity density pass: replace abstractions with the actual entity names.

The entity-dense version tells a human and creates a web of relationships that AI systems can extract — competitive positioning (Lululemon, Gymshark), product categories (leggings, sports bras), use cases (yoga, Pilates, gym), distribution channels (own site, Selfridges). Use the clean version for social bios with character limits. Use the entity-dense version for schema descriptions, Wikidata entries, and profile descriptions where space allows.

When narratives evolve

Narratives have a shelf life. A tech company that encoded "connected workspace" across all surfaces six months ago may now be positioning around AI agents — and every previously-aligned surface has become a new fragmentation source. A DTC brand that launches a men's line. A restaurant that shifts from casual to fine dining. When the canonical narrative changes, the entire Audit and Encode cycle needs to rerun.

The faster a business moves, the more frequently Structure needs to refresh. Watch for these triggers: major product pivot, new positioning language on the homepage, new customer world identified in Know, market expansion, acquisition. Each one means the narratives defined here need revisiting — and every surface encoded against the old narrative needs updating.

Where these feed

These narratives feed both directions. Back into Map — where they sharpen the Narrative component row in each context map. The Narrative row is no longer just "create a hub page" but "deploy this specific positioning on this surface." Forward into encoding — where the company-level narrative becomes your schema description, your Wikidata entry, and every profile bio. Customer-world narratives become collection page descriptions, audience landing page schema, and the voice for content in that customer world.

Storing and Managing the Narratives

The canonical narratives need a home — a single document that everyone references, not a slide deck buried in someone's inbox. The tool matters less than the access pattern: one URL, one source of truth, visible version history, and the ability for relevant teams to comment and suggest.

Section 1: Current Narratives
Always at the top. The company-level narrative (entity-dense version), each customer-world narrative with its competitive anchor, and the date each was last approved. This is the section people bookmark. When someone needs to write a G2 profile, update a social bio, or brief an agency, they start here.
Section 2: Narrative History
A running log of meaningful shifts. Each entry captures: the date, what changed, why it changed (the trigger — product pivot, new customer world, repositioning), and who approved it. Not a changelog for every word edit — just the strategic shifts. When the company moves from "connected workspace" to "AI-powered workspace with agents," that's an entry. When the DTC brand launches a men's line and adds a new customer world, that's an entry. This history becomes valuable when teams need to understand why the narrative changed — and when new hires need context on the brand's evolution.
Section 3: Surface Registry
A table listing every surface where the narrative is deployed: the surface name, who owns it (social team, SEO, product marketing, front-of-house manager), the date it was last aligned, and its current status (Consistent / Needs Update / Stale). This turns the consistency audit from a periodic project into a living tracker. When a new narrative version is approved, every row in the surface registry flips to "Needs Update" — and the row owners are responsible for bringing their surface into alignment.
Who owns it
One person or a very small group has final approval on narrative changes. In a SaaS company, this is typically a brand lead or head of marketing. In a DTC brand, it might be the founder or creative director. In a restaurant group, the owner or managing partner. In an agency, the managing director. Other teams — product, sales, community, front-of-house — can suggest changes through comments. But the canonical version is controlled, not crowdsourced. Distributed input, centralized approval. The surface registry can have distributed ownership (the social team owns the TikTok bio, the SEO team owns schema, the restaurant manager owns the Google Business Profile), but the narrative itself has a single approver.

Put it where people already work. If the team lives in Google Workspace, it's a Google Doc with a linked Sheet for the surface registry. In Notion, it's a wiki page with a database for the registry. In Microsoft, it's a SharePoint doc with a linked Excel tracker. Don't create a new system for this. The narratives need to live where the people who reference them — the social media manager updating a bio, the SEO writing schema, the account manager briefing a partner — will actually find them.

Step 2: The Consistency Audit

Now you know what you should be saying. The next step is finding out how far reality has drifted. The consistency audit is embarrassingly simple and almost nobody does it: collect your brand description from every surface, read them back to back, and mark the contradictions.

Surface Inventory by Industry

Start with the surfaces your customer worlds use (captured in Know). Then add the entity databases that AI systems read directly. Not every business needs every surface — a local restaurant doesn't need Crunchbase, a SaaS company probably doesn't need TikTok. The surfaces that matter are the ones where your customers and AI agents encounter you.

The Read-Through
Collect
Copy your brand description from every surface on your inventory into a single document. Include the platform name next to each one. For social platforms with character limits, capture the full bio text.
Read back to back
Read every description in sequence. Out loud if possible. You're looking for the feeling of reading about the same company versus the feeling of reading about different companies that happen to share a name.
Mark four things
Contradictions — surfaces that describe you differently. Staleness — descriptions written years ago that no longer reflect what you do. Absences — surfaces with no description at all, or platform defaults. Category mismatches — platforms that categorize you in different categories (this one is especially damaging for entity clarity).
Score
For each surface: does this echo the canonical narrative? A simple Consistent / Partial / Missing / Wrong assessment is enough. The goal isn't a detailed report — it's a clear picture of where the gaps are, ordered by the surface's importance to your customer worlds.
Common Fragmentation Patterns
Category Contradictions
G2 says "project management." Wikidata says "note-taking application." Capterra says "collaboration tool." AI systems encounter three different answers to "what is this?" The entity graph fragments.
Description Staleness
Crunchbase still has the Series A pitch. LinkedIn About was written by a departed CMO. Trustpilot shows the platform default. The descriptions are technically present but describe a company that no longer exists.
Missing Presences
No Wikidata entry. Unclaimed Knowledge Panel. Empty YouTube About section. These aren't inconsistencies — they're gaps where AI systems look for you and find nothing. Silence is a signal too.
Voice Drift
LinkedIn sounds corporate. TikTok sounds casual. G2 sounds enterprise. Your site sounds startup. Each was written by a different team for a different audience. The question: would an AI reading all four know it's the same company?
What about mentions you don't control?

The audit focuses on controlled surfaces — your site, your profiles, your Wikidata entry, your schema. These are where you fix fragmentation. But your brand also appears on surfaces you didn't write: blog roundups, podcast interviews, Reddit threads, listicle features, news coverage.

Don't chase these. Organic third-party mentions are valuable to AI systems precisely because they're independent. A Reddit comment explaining why someone switched to your product carries more entity weight than a self-published description because it's independent verification. Trying to control how every external source describes you is unreasonable at scale and counterproductive — you'd be trying to manage signals that are valuable because they're unmanaged.

The practical distinction: Controlled surfaces (your profiles, your schema) — fix the fragmentation here, this is Structure's job. Semi-controlled surfaces (your podcast appearances, guest articles, conference talks) — use your canonical narrative in future appearances, this is a Publish concern. Uncontrolled surfaces (third-party mentions, organic discussions) — measure these in Practice 5, don't manage them. The one exception: if a major authoritative source (Wikipedia, an industry database) has something factually wrong, correct it through their editorial process.

The audit is the highest-leverage action

The consistency audit and the fixes that follow it produce more entity improvement than any schema implementation. Most teams skip straight to technical markup. The ones that start with "are we even telling the same story everywhere?" find the biggest gaps before writing a line of JSON-LD. Fix the fragmentation before you encode the foundation.

Step 3: Encode

The audit shows where reality has drifted from your canonical narratives. Encoding fixes the gaps and builds the machine-readable infrastructure that AI systems use to parse your brand. The priority sequence is the same regardless of industry — what changes is which surfaces carry the most weight and where the effort concentrates.

Priority 1 — Highest Leverage
Fix the Fragmentation
Update all surface descriptions to use your canonical narratives. Request category corrections on platforms you don't fully control. Adapt the narrative for platform constraints (character limits on social bios) while maintaining the core identity.
What this looks like: Rewriting your G2 company description. Updating your Crunchbase profile. Aligning your social bios. Refreshing your Trustpilot company page. Contacting G2 about a category mismatch. Compressing your canonical narrative into 80 characters for TikTok without losing what you are.
Priority 2 — On-Site Foundation
Schema Markup
Organization schema with your canonical description. sameAs links connecting to every external presence. Type-appropriate schema: Product for e-commerce, LocalBusiness for brick-and-mortar, SoftwareApplication for tools.
Why sameAs matters: This is the explicit signal to AI systems that your website, your G2 profile, your YouTube channel, and your Wikidata entry are all the same entity. Without sameAs, AI systems infer the connection. With it, the connection is declared. List every external URL where your brand has a profile.
Priority 3 — Knowledge Graph
Wikidata, Wikipedia, Knowledge Panel
Wikidata: create or update your entry with correct properties — "instance of," official website, industry, key people, products, integrations. This directly feeds Google's Knowledge Graph. Wikipedia: assess eligibility (not every brand qualifies, and this is not a marketing exercise). Knowledge Panel: claim and verify through Google Search Console.
For newer or smaller brands: Wikidata eligibility depends on notability. If your brand doesn't qualify yet, focus on the surfaces you do control — complete Google Merchant Center data, rich Google Business Profile, claimed social profiles. The Knowledge Graph path opens as the brand grows. Don't force it.
Priority 4 — Compounds Over Time
Profile Completeness on Key Surfaces
Go beyond descriptions. Fill every available field on your key platforms: features, integrations, screenshots, pricing tiers, categories, contact information. A complete profile signals entity maturity to AI systems. An empty profile with a name and one-line description signals minimal presence.
Surface selection from Know: The surfaces that matter are the ones your customer worlds use. A SaaS company might prioritize G2 and Capterra feature completeness. A DTC brand might prioritize Google Merchant Center product data. A restaurant might prioritize Google Business Profile attributes (cuisine type, price range, dining options, hours).
Where the Effort Concentrates

The priority sequence (fix fragmentation, encode schema, build Knowledge Graph presence, complete profiles) is universal. What changes is where the heavy lifting sits.

B2B SaaS DTC / E-Commerce Services / Hospitality
Highest-leverage single surface G2 / Capterra profile alignment Product schema across catalog Google Business Profile
Biggest typical gap Wikidata staleness or absence Social bio fragmentation Multi-location inconsistency
Heaviest encoding work Review platform monitoring (moderate) Product feed completeness — hundreds of SKUs (massive) Multi-platform profile management (moderate to heavy)
Industry-specific priority Crunchbase, StackShare, integration marketplaces Google Merchant Center, collection page schema Apple Maps, reservation platforms, local directories

Notice the effort disparity. A SaaS company's encoding work is concentrated on a handful of high-value surfaces — maybe 10–15 profiles and one website's schema. A DTC brand with 200 products needs Product schema on every product page, consistent collection page schema, and complete Google Merchant Center data. A multi-location restaurant group needs a fully built Google Business Profile for every location. The priority sequence is the same. The scale of execution is very different.

The Handoff

Structure defines and encodes your identity. Publish deploys it. You're delivering two things forward: canonical narratives at every level (the source of truth for how your brand talks about itself in content, community participation, and review responses) and a consistent entity foundation (the infrastructure that all content is published onto).

The distinction matters. Structure answers "can AI systems parse what we are?" Publish answers "is our story out there, everywhere it needs to be?" Structure gives you an identity that's consistent and machine-readable. Publish gives you a presence that's rich, authentic, and covering the surfaces where your customer worlds make decisions.

The question that connects them: "Now that AI systems can accurately parse what we are, is our brand narrative present and consistent across every surface where customers and AI agents encounter us?"

What This Changes

Brand consistency became an SEO deliverable
AI search made every surface where your brand is described an input to your visibility. Your Trustpilot profile, your TikTok bio, your Wikidata entry — these were never SEO concerns before. They are now. The teams that coordinate across surfaces will have stronger entity signals than the teams that optimize their website in isolation.
The highest-leverage action is the least technical
The one-paragraph narrative exercise and the consistency audit together produce more entity improvement than any schema implementation. Most teams skip straight to markup. The ones that start with "are we even telling the same story everywhere?" find the biggest gaps before writing a line of JSON-LD.
Different industries, same priority sequence
The specific surfaces differ (G2 vs. Trustpilot vs. Google Business Profile). The encoding effort differs (5 profiles vs. 500 product pages). But the sequence is universal: define the narrative, audit cross-surface consistency, fix the fragmentation, encode schema, build Knowledge Graph presence, complete profiles. The process works for a SaaS company, a DTC brand, a restaurant chain, and a consulting firm.
Structure is infrastructure, not a project
Structure operates at three cadences, and most teams only do the first. Initial setup: Define, Audit, Encode for the first time. This is where most teams stop. Drift maintenance: periodic re-audits (quarterly or semi-annual) to catch surfaces where other teams updated descriptions with inconsistent language, new profiles that were created without referencing the canonical narrative, or descriptions that went stale. Pivot refresh: triggered by significant business evolution — new product positioning, new customer worlds, market expansion, acquisition. A tech company that repositions around AI agents after building its entity identity around "connected workspace" needs to rerun the full cycle. Every surface encoded against the old narrative becomes a new fragmentation source the moment the narrative moves. The faster the business evolves, the more frequently Structure needs to refresh.
You can't encode what you haven't defined
This is the most common failure mode. Teams implement Organization schema with a generic meta description. They create a Wikidata entry with a stale tagline. They update their G2 profile with whatever the product marketer had in a slide deck. The encoding is only as good as the narrative behind it. If the narrative doesn't exist — or if three different versions of it live in three different departments — the encoding just amplifies the fragmentation.
Encoding affects retrieval immediately but training data on its own timeline
AI systems encounter your brand through two mechanisms. Retrieval — real-time web searches that read your current surfaces as they are right now. When ChatGPT searches the web to answer a question about your brand, it reads your current G2 profile, your current schema, your current Trustpilot page. This is the mechanism Structure's encoding work most directly affects, and it's where you see results first. Training data — the corpus the model was trained on, which reflects a snapshot in time and updates on its own cycle (months to years between major runs). If your Wikidata entry said "note-taking application" when training data was collected, the model may carry that association regardless of today's corrections. The trend is toward more retrieval and grounding, which means the surfaces you control and encode today carry increasing weight. But the honest framing is: consistent, entity-dense signals across controlled surfaces compound over time across both mechanisms. You build the picture AI systems assemble whether they're searching live or drawing from training data — but the live layer is where the encoding work pays off first.
Entity SEO is the tactical layer beneath this practice
The five optimization strategies from entity SEO — entity foundation assessment, query decomposition testing, competitive entity mapping, entity-dense content, and strategic co-citations — are the implementation playbook for the encoding step. Structure sets the strategy and infrastructure. Entity SEO techniques operationalize the encoding.
