Why this page exists

Not a plan to leave anything or become anything. A mirror. The exercise: build the profile of the best possible advisor in a world where Google automates the execution layer — then hold it up against my own skills to see what's already there and what's missing.

Google Marketing Live 2026 made the premise concrete. Philipp Schindler opened with "after today, everything is AI search," and the whole keynote pointed one direction — hand the execution to Gemini. If the machine now runs the campaigns, the question worth answering is: what's the human still for? This page is the answer, drawn so the blank spots are obvious.

The role Google just rewrote

For fifteen years the valuable platform consultant was a channel technician — someone whose edge was knowing where the levers were and pulling them better than you could. GML 2026 is Google announcing it will pull the levers itself. Ask Advisor runs the account. Asset Studio writes the creative. PMax and the new AI Search formats bid and place automatically. The technician's edge is the exact thing being automated.

So the value moves. It doesn't disappear — it relocates to the two ends the machine can't be trusted to own: the inputs it works from and the verification of whether it worked. Google's agent optimises toward Google's definition of success, not your profit. Someone independent has to brief it correctly and check its homework.

The skill stack — six layers

This advisor isn't one skill done deeply. It's six layers stacked, running from the business reality at the top, down into the machine, and back up to proof. The order matters: each layer below serves the one above it. The number on the meter is profit, and everything else is in service of moving it.

01
Financial fluency — reads a P&L like a native language
Thinks in contribution margin, CAC, LTV, payback period, and cash — not blended ROAS. Knows that "good ROAS" can still lose money, and that the only question that matters is incremental profit. This is the layer that lets you sit across from a founder or CFO as a peer, not a vendor.
02
Measurement & causality — separates correlation from cause
Designs incrementality tests, geo experiments, and holdouts. Reads and builds marketing mix models — Google folded Meridian into GA360 this year because measurement is the real battleground. Treats every platform-reported conversion as a claim to verify, not a fact to report.
03
Google Ads operational excellence — knows what the machine is doing
Auction mechanics, campaign architecture, PMax and Demand Gen, Merchant Center and feed quality, bidding strategy, the new AI Search ad formats. Not to push buttons forever — to know the machine well enough to catch it when it's wrong and steer it when it drifts.
04
AI & agent orchestration — directs the labour
Briefs Gemini and Ask Advisor the way you'd brief a sharp junior. Builds tooling and automations around the platform. Treats the agent as labour to direct, audit, and correct — never as an oracle to obey. As execution gets automated, this is the layer that replaces hours of manual work.
05
Search & visibility — owns the whole surface, paid and organic
Understands intent, demand, and how brand presence across surfaces feeds the paid machine. Paid and organic are collapsing into one AI-search surface; fluency in both is the whole-picture advantage that channel specialists can't match.
06
Editorial rigour & creative taste — writes the brief, judges the output
Asset Studio can generate a thousand variants; taste decides which message is true to the business and which one lands. When creative production becomes free, judgment about creative becomes the scarce thing. This is the layer that briefs the machine and edits what it returns.

Is it even feasible to pack six skills into one person?

The honest answer: the profile is rare precisely because it's hard — but not for the reason it looks. No single layer is impossibly difficult. The difficulty is that these layers usually live in different people. Finance owns layer one. A media buyer owns three and four. An SEO owns five. A creative owns six. And almost nobody owns layer two. Stacking them in one head is the whole moat.

Feasibility turns on one distinction: fluency versus mastery. You don't need world-class mastery in all six. You need mastery in the judgment layers — financial fluency, measurement, editorial taste — and working fluency in the operational ones, because the machine now supplies the operational depth. That's the gift the agentic shift hands you: it lowers the mastery bar on execution and raises the premium on judgment.

There's a second distinction that decides feasibility for any specific person: slow skills versus fast skills. Financial fluency and editorial taste take years and can't be crammed. Google operations and measurement can be built in months. So the profile is most feasible for someone who already owns the slow layers — and is missing only the fast ones.

The thing worth noticing
The slow, can't-cram skills — P&L fluency, narrative, editorial taste — are weighted toward the enterprise end, which is also the highest-fee end. The buildable skills — Google ops, measurement tooling — are most needed at the SMB and DTC end. So the foundation that takes years already points up-market; the gaps that take months point down-market. You can earn while you build by starting where the gaps are cheapest to close.

The read
Three layers owned — one of them at a level few reach — one partial and nearly there, and two genuine gaps. The decisive part: the two gaps are the fast ones. The slow, expensive, can't-be-crammed layers are already in place. The build is two technical skills layered on top of a foundation that took fifteen years to pour. That's what makes this specific profile unusually feasible for this specific person.

The stance

You don't sell Google Ads management. You sell the number. The agent is the labour. The P&L is the scoreboard. The proof is the product.

The channel technician sells hours and reports ROAS. The Outcome Operator sells incremental profit and proves causation. One of those is being automated this year. The other gets more valuable every time the machine gets better — because a better machine makes judgment, not button-pushing, the scarce thing in the room.
